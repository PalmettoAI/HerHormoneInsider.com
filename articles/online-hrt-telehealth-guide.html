Online HRT in 2026: every legitimate telehealth pathway, ranked by what they actually prescribe

A telehealth visit cannot replace a hematologist. But for the 80% of perimenopausal women who need a transdermal estradiol patch, micronized progesterone, and a clinician who knows the difference between Provera and Prometrium — it is now the fastest, often cheapest path to evidence-based hormone therapy in America.

Sarah Wexler · Senior editor, with clinical review by Dr. Lena Marchetti, MD
Updated April 18, 2026
Medically reviewed

Until 2020, the standard pathway to hormone replacement therapy ran through an in-network OB/GYN. The well-documented problem: most US OB/GYN training programs allocate fewer than 30 hours of menopause education across four years of residency. The predictable outcome: most women who walk in describing classic perimenopausal symptoms walk out with a referral, an SSRI, or a pat on the arm.

The rise of online HRT via menopause-focused telehealth has solved approximately half of that problem. The visits are with NCMP-certified clinicians (largely). The prescribing is informed by 2022-era evidence, not 2002 WHI fear (largely). And the cost is, in many cases, less than the copay on the OB/GYN visit you spent six weeks waiting for.

Who telehealth HRT works for — and who it does not

Telehealth menopause platforms are best suited to:

  • Healthy women aged 38–60 with classic vasomotor, sleep, mood, or GSM complaints
  • No personal history of estrogen-sensitive cancer, VTE, or stroke
  • An existing primary care relationship for routine annual screening (mammogram, lipid panel, blood pressure)

They are not the right venue for women with complex risk profiles (BRCA carriers, prior breast cancer, recent clot history) or anyone who needs procedural care like an IUD insertion, endometrial biopsy, or ablation. For those situations, our perimenopause guide covers how to find a NAMS-certified specialist locally.

What separates a good telehealth platform from a bad one

The five criteria we used to score every provider in our full comparison table:

  1. Prescribing range: do they offer transdermal estradiol, micronized progesterone, vaginal estrogen, and testosterone? Or only oral pills?
  2. Clinician quality: NCMP certification, length of visit, ability to message the same clinician across visits.
  3. Price transparency: published pricing for both visit and medication, or quote-on-intake?
  4. Insurance handling: cash-pay, in-network with major payers, or both?
  5. Exit terms: how easy to cancel, transfer prescriptions, or download records?

The legitimate providers, briefly

This section is intentionally short — the full numerical comparison is in our side-by-side provider table.

  • Midi Health — broadest insurance acceptance, NCMP-certified clinicians, full prescribing range. Best for insurance users.
  • Alloy Women's Health — physician-only, transparent flat pricing, mature operations. Best for cash-pay users who want simplicity.
  • Evernow — strong on the off-label testosterone conversation, fast intake. Best if libido / energy is the primary complaint.
  • Joi Women's Wellness — closer to a wellness brand than a clinic; useful but read the fine print on labs and contraindication screening.
  • Winona — bioidentical-only, compounded preparations heavy. Useful if a major retail pharmacy can't fulfill, but compounding always reduces dosing precision.

Cost ranges in 2026

Initial visit: $99–$249 self-pay. Monthly recurring: $25–$110 depending on whether medications are bundled. With insurance, total monthly cost can fall under $30 for the medication-only line items. The detailed pharmacy-level breakdown is in our HRT cost and insurance guide.

What to ask before you submit your intake form

  • Is the prescriber NCMP-certified, or do they hold equivalent menopause training?
  • What is the default first-line regimen they prescribe for vasomotor symptoms?
  • Is transdermal estradiol available, or only oral?
  • Will they prescribe vaginal estrogen alongside systemic therapy?
  • Will they consider testosterone for HSDD if appropriate?
  • If I cancel, can I download my chart and have prescriptions transferred?
Red flag Any "telehealth" service that prescribes hormone therapy without reviewing baseline labs (lipid panel, fasting glucose, complete blood count, TSH) and asking about your cancer family history is not practicing medicine. It is filling orders. Walk away.

The bottom line

For a healthy woman with garden-variety perimenopausal symptoms, the right telehealth provider is faster, cheaper, and frequently more evidence-based than the local OB/GYN she has waited two months to see. For a woman with a complex risk profile, telehealth is the wrong starting point. The art is knowing which one you are.
