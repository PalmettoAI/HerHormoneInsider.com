Perimenopause symptoms and treatment: a guide for women whose doctors keep saying everything looks fine

The bloodwork is normal. The pelvic exam is unremarkable. You are also forty-three, not sleeping, crying in parking lots, and forgetting the names of your coworkers. Both of those things can be true. One of them is probably perimenopause.

Dr. Lena Marchetti, MD · OB/GYN, NCMP-certified
Reviewed by Dr. Susan Reed, MD · April 22, 2026
Medically reviewed

The average woman in the United States visits seven clinicians and waits four years before someone names what is happening to her as perimenopause. The North American Menopause Society's 2024 practice survey found that fewer than 20% of OB/GYNs feel "very confident" prescribing menopausal hormone therapy, and only 6.8% of internal medicine residents receive any structured menopause education at all [1].

This is not a failure of diligence on your part. It is a failure of curriculum. What follows is the field guide we wish your primary care doctor had — the symptoms, the mechanism, the treatment landscape, and the specific language that gets a useful answer in a twelve-minute appointment.

What perimenopause actually is

Perimenopause is the seven-to-ten-year endocrine transition that ends with menopause — the day twelve consecutive months have passed without a menstrual period. It typically begins between ages 40 and 44, though one in five women enter it in their late thirties [2].

The defining feature is not low estrogen. It is erratic estrogen. As ovarian follicle reserves decline, estradiol production becomes wildly inconsistent — a single cycle can swing from near-postmenopausal levels to 2–3x the levels of a 30-year-old. Progesterone, by contrast, declines almost monotonically because anovulatory cycles increase. The result is a fluctuating estrogen-to-progesterone ratio with no reliable single-blood-draw signature, which is why a spot FSH or estradiol panel is a near-useless diagnostic tool in this window.

What to know A normal FSH or estradiol level does not rule out perimenopause. Diagnosis is clinical — based on cycle changes plus symptoms — not laboratory. If a clinician has dismissed you on the strength of "normal" hormone labs alone, that is not current evidence-based practice.

The 34 symptoms — and the hormonal mechanism behind each cluster

The Greene Climacteric Scale and the Menopause Rating Scale together catalog more than thirty distinct perimenopausal symptoms. Most fall into five mechanistic clusters:

Vasomotor (heat-regulation) symptoms

Hot flashes, night sweats, and the temperature dysregulation patients describe as "internal weather." Mechanism: estrogen withdrawal narrows the thermoneutral zone in the hypothalamus, so small changes in core body temperature trigger oversized peripheral vasodilation. Affects 75–80% of women in some form.

Sleep and mood

Early-morning waking, anxiety that is qualitatively different from prior anxiety, depression in women with no prior history, brain fog and word-finding difficulty. Mechanism: progesterone is a positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors — when it falls, the same receptor system implicated in benzodiazepine action loses tonic support. Estradiol modulates serotonin and dopamine reuptake; its fluctuations produce mood symptoms that respond more reliably to hormone therapy than to SSRIs in head-to-head trials [3].

Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM)

Vaginal dryness, painful intercourse, recurrent UTIs, urinary urgency. Mechanism: estrogen receptors throughout the vaginal epithelium, urethra, and bladder trigone atrophy without sustained estradiol. GSM is one of the most successfully treated perimenopausal complaints — local vaginal estrogen reverses it in 4–8 weeks — and one of the most under-prescribed.

Musculoskeletal

Joint pain, frozen shoulder (incidence peaks at exactly age 50–53), muscle loss, and the early acceleration of bone density loss that begins 1–2 years before the final menstrual period. Estrogen is anti-inflammatory in synovial tissue and anabolic in bone; its loss is felt structurally.

Metabolic and cardiovascular

Visceral fat redistribution, new-onset insulin resistance, rising LDL and apoB, and the loss of the pre-menopausal cardiovascular protection women carry. Cardiovascular event rates begin to converge with male peers within five years of the final period.

Treatment options: what the evidence supports in 2026

Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT / HRT)

The 2022 NAMS Position Statement and the 2024 Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline both endorse hormone therapy as first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms in women under 60 or within 10 years of their final menstrual period [4]. The drug landscape has changed dramatically since the 2002 Women's Health Initiative results most clinicians still mentally reference:

  • Transdermal estradiol (patch, gel, spray) bypasses first-pass hepatic metabolism and does not measurably increase venous thromboembolism risk in observational data — unlike the conjugated equine estrogens used in the WHI.
  • Micronized progesterone (Prometrium, generic) is bioidentical and structurally distinct from medroxyprogesterone acetate (Provera). It is sleep-promoting, taken at bedtime, and not associated with the breast cancer signal seen with synthetic progestins in the WHI.
  • Vaginal estrogen (cream, tablet, ring) treats GSM with negligible systemic absorption. The FDA's "black box" warning on local estrogen is widely considered outdated; NAMS, ACOG, and the British Menopause Society have all formally argued for its removal.
  • Testosterone for women remains off-label in the United States but is endorsed by the Global Consensus Position Statement (2019) for hypoactive sexual desire disorder. Compounded transdermal cream at female-physiological doses (typically 1–5 mg/day) is the most common formulation.

Non-hormonal pharmacologic options

For women who cannot or choose not to use hormones: low-dose paroxetine (Brisdelle) is FDA-approved for vasomotor symptoms; venlafaxine and gabapentin have decent vasomotor evidence; the new fezolinetant (Veozah, NK3 receptor antagonist, FDA-approved 2023) targets the hypothalamic mechanism directly without hormones.

Behavioral and supplement evidence

Cognitive behavioral therapy has Level A evidence for vasomotor symptoms — comparable to non-hormonal pharmacology. Black cohosh, soy isoflavones, and most "menopause" supplements have null-to-modest results in well-designed trials. Save the money.

The telehealth pathway: what it is, when it works, when it doesn't

The rise of menopause-focused telehealth (Midi, Alloy, Evernow, Joi, Hers, Winona, and several others) represents the largest expansion of access to online HRT prescription in the history of the indication. We covered the full landscape in our comparison of telehealth HRT providers; the short version:

  • Best for: women with classic perimenopausal symptoms, no contraindications (active estrogen-receptor-positive cancer, recent VTE, active liver disease), and a primary care doctor who can do annual screening.
  • Not appropriate for: women with complex risk profiles (BRCA carriers, history of breast cancer, prior stroke or VTE), or those who need an IUD, ablation, or any procedural management.
  • Cost expectation: $50–$110/month for the visit + medication bundle, depending on provider. We track current pricing in our cost and insurance breakdown.

What to ask a provider — the script

Whether your visit is in-person or telehealth, the questions below produce a substantively different appointment than "I think I might be in perimenopause." Print this. Take it with you.

  1. "Based on my symptoms and cycle pattern, do you think I'm in perimenopause?"
  2. "Are you NCMP-certified, or comfortable prescribing hormone therapy in the perimenopausal window?"
  3. "What is your default first-line approach for vasomotor symptoms in a 43-year-old with no history of breast cancer or VTE?"
  4. "Would you start with transdermal estradiol or oral? What's your reasoning?"
  5. "If I have a uterus, what progestogen do you typically pair? Synthetic or micronized?"
  6. "Do you prescribe vaginal estrogen for GSM, even alongside systemic HRT?"
  7. "What labs would you actually want, and what would you ignore?"
  8. "What is your follow-up cadence in the first six months?"
If a provider responds to question 3 with "I'd start with an SSRI," they are practicing 2008 medicine. That is not a moral failing — it is an information gap. Politely thank them and request a referral to a NAMS-certified menopause specialist.

Cost expectations in 2026

Approximate ranges, US, in-network or self-pay:

  • OB/GYN visit (insurance, in-network): $25–$60 copay
  • Telehealth menopause visit (self-pay): $99–$249 initial, $25–$70 monthly thereafter
  • Generic transdermal estradiol patch (28-day supply): $15–$45 with GoodRx; $0–$15 with insurance
  • Micronized progesterone 100mg (30-day supply): $25–$60 with GoodRx; $5–$25 with insurance
  • Vaginal estradiol cream (per 42.5g tube): $40–$200 depending on insurance and brand
  • Compounded testosterone cream (1 month): $50–$120 self-pay through telehealth

The bottom line

Perimenopause is a clinical diagnosis, not a lab finding. The treatment landscape is meaningfully better than what most women are told. The provider who dismisses you is not necessarily a bad doctor — they are a doctor who graduated before menopause was meaningfully taught, and who has not yet caught up. You are allowed to seek the one who has.
