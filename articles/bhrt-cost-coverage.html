What HRT actually costs in 2026 — with and without insurance

"Bioidentical hormones" sounds expensive. Most of them aren't. The cost gap between a $9 monthly estradiol patch and a $190 compounded "BHRT pellet" is mostly marketing — and almost entirely reversible if you know what to ask for.

Sarah Wexler · Senior editor
Updated April 11, 2026

The short answer

For most women in 2026, all-in monthly cost for evidence-based HRT — visit + estradiol + progesterone — falls between $15 and $110, depending on whether you use insurance and what telehealth platform you choose. Compounded "pellet therapy" routinely runs $300–$600 per quarter, with no clinical advantage and notable dosing-precision concerns.

Cost of the visit itself

  • In-network OB/GYN: $25–$60 copay, plus any lab fees
  • NAMS-certified menopause specialist (in-network): $40–$90 copay
  • Telehealth menopause initial: $99–$249 self-pay (Midi: in-network with most major payers)
  • Telehealth menopause monthly: $25–$110 bundled, or $0–$45 visits-only

If you have a high-deductible plan and your OB/GYN visit will count toward your deductible, the math sometimes favors paying cash for telehealth. We covered the calculus in our online HRT telehealth guide.

"BHRT" pellets, compounded creams, and the markup problem

"Bioidentical" is a marketing word, not an FDA classification. Estradiol is bioidentical. So is micronized progesterone. They are also FDA-approved, mass-produced, and cheap. The category called "BHRT" in clinic marketing usually refers to compounded preparations — pellets implanted under the skin, custom creams, sublingual troches — that are not FDA-evaluated for dosing precision and that typically cost 4–10x the FDA-approved equivalents.

NAMS, ACOG, and the Endocrine Society all recommend FDA-approved bioidentical hormone therapy over compounded preparations, on the basis of dose accuracy, batch consistency, and absence of a published efficacy advantage for compounding. If a clinic is recommending pellets at the first visit, that is informative.

Worth knowing The same molecule — estradiol — is sold both as an FDA-approved patch ($15/month) and as a compounded pellet ($400/quarter). The compounded version is not more "natural." It is the same hormone, less reliably dosed.

Getting your insurance to cover more

  • Use generics. Estradiol patch generics are essentially universally covered; brand names trigger step therapy.
  • Ask for the 90-day fill. Mail-order pharmacy through your insurer's pharmacy benefit usually drops monthly cost meaningfully.
  • Vaginal estrogen has Medicare coverage — recently expanded — and is on most commercial formularies. The cream form is often more expensive than the tablet or ring.
  • Testosterone is rarely covered for women, since the indication is off-label. Compounded transdermal cream from a telehealth provider is the typical workaround.

The bottom line

If you are paying more than $110 per month for HRT in 2026 and you do not have a clinical reason for compounded therapy, you are paying for branding. The same molecules in FDA-approved form are an order of magnitude cheaper and better-dosed. Start there.
